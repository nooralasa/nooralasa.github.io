Thesis

Born in Zarqa, Jordan, I spent the first decade of my life between Jordan and the West Bank. At the age of 13, I moved to Madaba, Jordan to board at King's Academy, the first New England style boarding school in the Middle East. I moved to Cambridge, MA for college at the age of 17, and have been living there since then.

I graduated from MIT in 2016 with a major in Computer Science and Engineering and a minor in Political Science. I then joined the Media Lab's Learning Initiative where I worked on the Refugee Learning Accelerator, receiving my MEng in Computer Science and Engineering from MIT in 2017. Currently, I'm a Senior Software Engineer at Oracle working on Cloud Commerce.

I'm a Software Engineer, a lifelong learner, and a public speaker. I'm passionate about entrepreneurship, social impact, and the practice of teaching.

  • Cambridge 2012

    MIT

    If King's Academy gave me confidence, idealism and a liberal arts education, MIT gave me humility, realism and technical competence. With a challenging technical curriculum, a student body full of overachievers, and a culture of tackling the toughest problems, I came to understand the complexities of the technology and intricacies of our modern age.

I learned about entrepreneurship, user-centric design, and Artificial Intelligence. I became a Teaching Assistant, learned about learning and digital learning technology. I learned French, traveled throughout Europe with MISTI, and participated in multiple Hackathons.

  • Madaba 2008

    King's Academy

    In 2008, I was one of a few SEP students in my cohort to get accepted into King's Academy and receive full Financial Aid. Ninth grade was the hardest year for me, as I struggled to adjust to a rigorous curriculum in English. Math was my favorite subject since it came naturally to me and did not have as much of a language barrier. King's Academy was also challenging socially. Coming from a modest upbringing, this was the first time for me to interact with others from a different social strata.

    Today, I look fondly on my time at King's: smart and motivated students, caring and invested teachers, beautiful campus and visionary leadership. I grew exponentially and took advantage of every opportunity the school exposed me to. I traveled outside of Jordan and Palestine for the first time in my life, attending Round Square conferences in Kenya, India and Thailand. I learned to play an instrument, ran a half marathon, lead the debate team, learned to code, won the declamations competition, and expanded my academic horizons. I started a community service initiative, taking three cohorts of King's students to visit my village in Palestine.

    On March 14, 2011, I got accepted into the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, starting a new chapter in my life.

  • Madaba 2006

    SEP

    In the fall of 2006, the principle at my public school in Sahab asked me to come to his office. He gave me an application that was sent to public schools around the Kingdom, for the Summer Enrichment Program at King's Academy. King's Academy was at the time being built as the first New England Style Boarding School in the region. It would open to its first class of students a year later in 2007. SEP was designed as an outreach program to serve under-privileged students in Jordan.

    For three summers, from 2006 until 2008, I would spend three weeks boarding at King's Academy. Living with volunteers from Jordan and the States, we focused on improving our English and engaged in a variety of extra-curricular activities. In those formative years, I started to learn English and was excited about the hopeful prospects that King's Academy represented.

  • Sahab 1999

    Moved to Sahab

    After my father returned from the UAE, we moved to Sahab, in East Amman. Using his savings from working in the UAE and a generous loan from relatives, my father purchased land in Sahab and constructed a house for the family. My parents spent the next two decades of their lives repaying that loan. Coming from a family of teachers, education was key in our upward movement in society. We slowly saw our fortunes improve over the years as we entered the Jordanian middle class.

    Unlike Zarqa, where a large Palestinian refugee population lived, Sahab was a rising city with a Bedouin Jordanian population that settled there in the mid 19th century. Growing up in Sahab, I realized that my Palestinian identity was in conflict with my Jordanian one, and slowly learned to be proud of both identities and have them co-exist. I went to public schools in Sahab and was always at the top of my class. I lead the morning broadcasting club at my school.

  • Zarqa 1995

    Birth

    I was born in March of 1995 in Zarqa, Jordan. The youngest of six, a healthy baby, with cheeks as red as tomatoes. At the time, my father was working in Al-Ain in the UAE, where he taught for five years.

  • Zarqa 1983

    Family moves to Jordan

    In 1976, my father moved to Jordan to study at the University of Jordan. He later got married in Mas'ha, and in 1983 my mom moved with him to Zarqa, Jordan. They were both school teachers. My father taught at Public Schools, and later became a school principal. My mom taught at UNRWA schools in Jordan.

    Since they moved to Jordan before the disengagement of 1988, they became Jordanian citizens, and held yellow cards allowing them to visit the West Bank for family reunification, but preventing them from permanently residing there. As my parents were both school teachers, we would spend the school year in Jordan and summer vacation in the West Bank.

  • Mas'ha 1948

    From Kafr Qasim to Mas'ha

    Following the news of the Deir Yassin Massacre, the heads of the Sarsur family met. Fearing a similar fate to our village, Kafr Qassim, my great-grandfather decided to send his eldest son and his family to a near-by village called Mas'ha, to maintain the family line if the worst were to happen. My father's family similarly fled Kafr Qassim to Mas'ha. 700,000 Palestinians fled their villages or were forcibly expelled between 1947-1949, the events of which we commemorate as the Palestinian Nakba.

    In 1948, Kafr Qassim and Mas'ha, only 8 km apart, were permanently severed. Kafr Qasim came under Israeli Martial Law, and Mas'ha under Jordanian control until Israel occupied the West Bank and Gaza in 1967. My great-grandfather's fear later came to be true with the 1956 Kafr Qasim Massacre.

    To learn more about Palestine, checkout my Palestine 101 lecture series, or ask me about it directly.


Career

Professional Experience. View my LinkedIn.

During my 3+ years as a Software Developer at Oracle, I have participated in the design, development, and maintenance of industry-grade software. Working on Oracle Commerce Cloud (OCC), I spent the first eight months of my career learning iOS development and contributing to the Assisted Selling Application. I later transitioned to working on a redesign of OCC's main product line: the Storefront.

Large retailers and telecom companies use Oracle Commerce Cloud to build and customize their e-commerce sites. Customers purchase OCC to access an out-of-the-box, hosted commerce site and contract third-party implementation partners who use OCC's headless commerce API, development tooling, and sample UI widgets to customize the Storefront. In addition to the hosted storefront and the commerce sdk, customers get access to an Admin platform (for managing catalogs, site settings, etc), an Agent platform for supporting customer service and call centers, and the Assisted Selling App to manage commerce flows in-store.

During my first eight months at Oracle, I learned iOS development with Swift and XCode, and worked on adding features to ASA, including tax treatment and store credit implementations. At the beginning of 2017, my team transitioned its focus from working on the iOS application to a global initiative of rearchitecting our main product line: the Oracle Cloud Commerce Storefront. It began with a prototype from a team in Belfast for a framework using React and Server-Side Rendering with the OCC APIs, and with that was born the Open Storefront Framework (OSF).

The Open Storefront Framework (OSF) has three goals in mind: improving performance, improving SEO (Search Engine Optimization), and improving the third-party implementation partner's development process. My team in Cambridge became in charge of Marcus' (our persona for the implementation partner) development experience and integrating OSF with the existing architecture.

To improve Marcus' development experience, we relied on yarn workspaces and created a command line interface to interact with the OCC endpoints, sync assets, and push Marcus' developed application to our servers. In addition, we had to design a new architecture for installing and serving Marcus' application in the Oracle Cloud.

In its existing architecture, OCC ran in two Java Virtual Machines, one serving the OCC Admin API and its UI and another serving OCC Storefront (both API and UI). Adding React and Server-Side Rendering required adding a Presentation node.js Server to the existing architecture that talked to the Storefront API and got its site settings from Admin. A coworker and I designed and fully implemented a dockerized application that we call the deployment controller.

The deployment controller manages the lifecycle of a deployed node.js OCC application. It exposes a REST API to talk to OCC Admin, receives Marcus' customized OCC applications, and builds and runs them in a green/blue deployment model to ensure continual availability. In a nutshell, I've designed and built the system to run the new OSF Storefront in the Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI). The system that I've designed and built is currently running in OCI and serving customer storefronts. Through deploying the controller to OCI, I learned about Docker, Kubernetes, and other cloud technologies.

During those two years of developing the deployment controller and the logging solution for OSF, I grew accustomed to in-depth collaboration with a small team of four developers in Cambridge, as well as being the liaison for a global team distributed across three cities. I travelled to Belfast and Hyderabad to present our work in Cambridge and further that collaboration.

Given my natural affinity for presenting and public speaking, I became the go-to developer on the team to demo OSF internally and externally, including demoing the application in an early access session to implementation partners of OCC. I also worked with Oracle University and internal consultants on creating training for OCC developers. My work was recognized when I was promoted to Senior Software Engineer during my first two years at Oracle, an uncommon promotion at Oracle.


I worked at Vistaprint, summer of 2016, in Waltham Massachusetts. I joined the Product Design and Technology team and worked on a tool for country managers to add, remove, and edit dates on holiday calendars.

Project Description: Calendars generate substantial revenue for Vistaprint, particularly during the holiday season. While customers may customize and create calendar dates from Studio, all wall and desk calendars include a predetermined set of dates and date display behaviors. These are unique to each locale--week start, weekday names, and holidays all require accurate localization and translation review. The process for setting calendar date requirements has been prone to error; calendar dates are revisited and reviewed many times, by multiple stakeholders, annually. PDT developers are currently responsible for creating, modifying, and maintaining this data—which is inefficient for both developers and country managers. The goal of this project is to develop a full-stack web application by which regional experts may add, remove, and update calendar metadata. Our intention is to fully remodel the management workflow to eliminate developer involvement while also providing a guided and responsive self-service experience to our stakeholders.

Over the summer, I developed a standalone web service to help country managers track holiday dates. I designed a database schema, a RESTful web API, and a User Interface, and implemented them with postgreSQL/express/node.js/React stack. The service was deployed on AWS. While building the service, I practiced Design Thinking while working with relevant stakeholders from Country Managers, PDT Engineers, Product Owners and Localization Engineers. I also participated in agile development with daily stand-ups and using Kanban.

Research

Research Experience. Read my Master's Thesis.

Principal Investigator: Philipp Schmidt

Abstract: The Syrian refugee crisis has created serious challenges to higher education in regional host countries. While digital educational technologies from the West have the potential of mending some of these challenges, there is a lack of locally appropriate solutions. We see this as a reflection of the modest number of innovators who have the technical and pedagogical training for developing such technologies, with combined understanding of the refugee and regional context. We attempt to bridge this gap by developing LIME (Learning Innovators - Middle East), an online platform for a capacity building program in Jordan and Lebanon. LIME is a Progressive Web Application that was developed with a React/Redux/Node/PostgreSQL stack. Through LIME, learners can take online classes, gain valuable development skills and engage in a community of innovators, all the while working on projects to deal with the refugee crisis. We believe that our project-based, community-focused approach to digital learning can help learners in host countries gain the necessary skills to develop local solutions to refugee education. We hope that such an approach will also alleviate the stress on regional institutions of higher education. We embrace the challenge of refugee education as an opportunity to spur innovation in the Middle East.

Principal Investigator: Frédo Durand

Abstract: We seek to facilitate the authoring of handwritten educational video lectures and combine the advantages of vector graphics and video editing. Handwritten videos are currently recorded using video screen capture, which makes editing challenging. We believe that the power of digital content creation is in nonsequential iterative refinement. Teachers should be able to start a video lecture without a perfect script, and they should be able to improve their material over the year. To achieve this, we are working on developing Pentimento , an authoring software that dramatically facilitates the authoring and editing of handwritten video lectures. This project deals with the JavaScript version of Pentimento , to make it possible to create such videos in the browser and across multiple platforms.

Principal Investigator: Regina Barzilay

Abstract: Conflict zones are notorious for biased reporting under the monopoly of the controlling group and it is hard to get up-to-date and unbiased on the ground news. Social media platforms like Twitter utilize crowd sourced information from local people to provide updates in conflict zones. In this project, we apply NLP techniques to automate the process categorizing tweets regarding the Syrian conflict into discrete events that summarize the conflict over time. In particular, we extract faction, location and event type information from each tweet and summarize it on a time evolving map of Syria.

Principal Investigator: Josh Tenenbaum

Abstract: Previous research has shown how Bayesian models can be used to show how toddlers simultaneously infer property extension and sampling processes from small amounts of data (Gweon, Tenenbaum, & Schulz, 2009). In this project, we present a Church implementation of the model proposed in (Gweon, Tenenbaum, & Schulz, 2009). We then extend the experiments done in the paper to (1) examine how introducing an intent may a ect how adult learners infer property extension and (2) explore how priors on the properties may a ect how adults infer the property extension. Consistent with the predictions of the model, two behavioral experiments suggest that adults can rst infer the sampling process by observing the exper- imenter's intent, then use this information to infer the property extension.
